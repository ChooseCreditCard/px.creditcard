How Crypto Credit-Card Payments Actually Work

Crypto credit cards promise “spend your coins anywhere”. Here’s what really happens when you tap at the terminal — from conversion and spreads to rewards and tax questions.

Compare crypto-friendly credit cards

What is a Crypto Credit Card?

A crypto credit card looks and behaves like a normal Visa, Mastercard or similar, but is connected to a crypto wallet or exchange account. At the point of sale, your crypto is converted to fiat currency, which is used to settle the transaction with the card network.

Most products are not truly “on-chain at checkout”. Instead, the issuer or partner exchange sells a small amount of your crypto in real time or in batched lots, then uses fiat rails for settlement.

How CryptoPay Transactions Flow

When you tap or insert the card, the terminal sends a standard authorization request. Behind the scenes, the issuer or partner platform checks your crypto balance, reserves funds, converts to fiat and approves or declines the transaction.

Conversion may use a spot-rate at the time of purchase, plus a spread and possible extra fees. Some products convert proactively (preload), while others convert “just in time” for each transaction.

Fees, Spreads and Hidden Costs

Crypto cards can include exchange spreads, FX margins, network fees and issuer fees. Many marketing pages highlight rewards but downplay spreads on conversion, which can easily exceed 2–3% depending on liquidity and volatility.

Always read the fee schedule: look for explicit crypto-conversion fees, FX fees for foreign transactions, ATM withdrawal fees and any additional service charges from the linked exchange.

Rewards, Cashbacks and Token Incentives

Rewards may be paid as cash, points or native platform tokens. Some issuers offer higher rates when you stake a token or hold a minimum balance, but this concentrates risk in a single ecosystem.

Compare rewards after fees: a 3% token reward is less attractive if you are losing 2–3% on spreads and FX every time you use the card.

Risk, Volatility and Tax Considerations

Every time you convert crypto to fiat you may trigger a taxable event in your jurisdiction. That means daily spending could generate a large number of small taxable disposals.

Volatility can also work against you. If the market moves sharply between loading and spending, the real purchasing power of your holdings changes — regardless of the headline reward rate.
